MODULE 1: INTRODUCTION TO STRATEGY

Strategic management takes the view of the CEO or general manager and addresses the question of why some firms outperform others. Firm performance therefore lies at the core of strategic management as firms use their resources and adapt to their environments to help them win against rivals in the marketplace. To help understand what it takes for firms to succeed in today’s marketplace, this course will highlight how to assess the firm’s internal and external situation, to appreciate the elements of a successful strategy, and to understand the challenges of implementing such strategies under conditions of entrepreneurial uncertainty.

Learning Objectives. By the end of this module, students should be able to:

  1. Define strategic management and recognize its importance for professional and personal endeavors
  2. Describe the competitive landscape and explain how globalization and technological change can shape it
  3. Describe the work of strategic leaders.
  4. Explain the strategic management process

Big Question Question: What is Strategic Management and the Strategic Decision Making Process?

 

DEFINING STRATEGY

Strategy is an integrated and coordinated set of commitments and actions designed to leverage internal resources and core competencies that align with factors in the external environment to provide a firm with a competitive advantage. A firm achieves strategic competitiveness when it successfully formulates and implements a strategy that creates or adds value to its customers or interested stakeholders. Firms that implement this value-adding strategy that current and potential competitors are not simultaneously implementing and that competitors are unable to duplicate, or find too costly to imitate, achieve a sustainable competitive advantage (SCA). The field of strategic management explores the concept of relative firm performance, or why one firm outperforms another in the marketplace, and how they achieve this sustainable competitive advantage.

Startups or new ventures, often smaller and less established, usually measure their performance in ways other than profitability or above-average investor returns, such as the amount and speed of growth of new customers. These new ventures usually require more time to earn acceptable returns, but their success is often much more risky than established firms. Two main differences distinguish startups from more established firms and consequently increase the riskiness of their success: 1) Increased uncertainty and 2) Decreased resources. Because entrepreneurial startups are defined by doing something new or unproven, they often don’t have past history or adequate benchmarks with which to make strategic decisions or to measure success. In addition, startups don’t have the financial foundation or the reputational history of established firms to positively influence their business activities. As will be discussed further, however, these characteristics can be beneficial as entrepreneurial startups are able to be more nimble and flexible, allowing them to capitalize on changes in the marketplace more easily than large, bureaucratic firms.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What advantages and disadvantages do you think startups have over larger, more established firms?

 

One framework that can assist firms in their quest for a sustainable competitive advantage is the strategic management process, which encompasses the full set of commitments, decisions, and actions required for a firm to be successful. The three key elements of this process are analysis (inputs), formulation (decision making), and implementation (actions). The outcome of these elements is the overall performance of the firm, with iterative feedback mechanisms constantly informing each portion of the process.

21st CENTURY COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

The competitive landscape is one that changes and evolves rapidly, so strategic leaders need to ensure they are aware of the implications of these changes for their firms. Further, this change in the contemporary competitive landscape implies that traditional sources of competitive advantage – large, multi-industry firms with hundreds of thousands of employees, economies of scale, and huge advertising budgets – may not be as successful in the future as they were in the past. The new competitive mind-set must value flexibility, speed, innovation, integration, and the challenges as a result of constantly changing conditions.

In particular, two trends have had the biggest impact on the evolution of firms in the 21st century – globalization and rapid technological change. Governments, firms, and ordinary citizens are much more connected to their counterparts all over the world, with changing technology increasing the speed, reliability, and frequency of interactions. Supply chains and consumer markets are much more international than in the past, and as consumer interactions become more digital, traditional organizational structures are being redesigned.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What impacts have you seen in your own life from globalization and technological change? How have you seen this impacting the business activities of your favorite firms?

As a result of globalization and the spread of technology, competition will become more intense, which will manifest itself in some of the following ways:

  • Customers will continue to expect high levels of product quality at competitive prices
  • Easier access to information via technology will improve the knowledge of various actors in the market, including comparisons with other sources of goods and services
  • Global competition will continue to pressure companies to shorten product development lifecycles and to decrease timelines for new product and service introductions
  • Strategically competitive companies will be required to leverage insights learned both in domestic and global markets, modifying and adapting them as necessary in order to be successful
  • Foreign markets outside the United States will not only be home to cheaper sources of production and a market for consumer products, but will also be a provider of skilled human capital and increased innovation to compete with traditional global leaders

Similarly, as a result of the new competitive landscape, firms of all sizes must rethink how they can achieve a sustainable competitive advantage by positioning themselves to ask questions from a more global perspective to enable them to meet or exceed global standards. The importance of addressing the following questions becomes much greater as globalization and technological change increase:

  • Where should value-adding activities be performed?
  • Where are the most cost-effective markets for new capital?
  • Can products designed in one market be successfully adapted for sale in others?
  • How can we develop cooperative relationships or joint ventures with other firms that will enable us to capitalize on international growth opportunities?

 

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What firms have you seen adapt well to the globally competitive marketplace? Which ones have not done so?

 

STRATEGIC LEADERSHIP

The rapid and unpredictable technological change that characterizes this new globally competitive landscape also implies that strategic leaders must adopt new ways of thinking. Beyond simply being more entrepreneurial and innovative, leaders must also change the way they interact with their employees, since personal connections are even more important as digitization and electronic communication become more and more prevalent.

Strategic flexibility represents the set of capabilities – in all areas of their operations – that firms use to respond to the various demands and opportunities found in dynamic, uncertain environments. This implies that firms must develop certain capabilities, including the capacity to learn continuously, that will provide the firm with new skill sets. However, because inertia can build up over time that makes it harder to change strategies and update paradigms, those working within firms to develop strategic flexibility should understand the task is not an easy one. As a result, sometimes firms’ past strategies and core competencies create barriers for change and reduce strategic flexibility. Strategic leaders who are capable of rapidly and broadly applying what they learn are more likely to keep pace with the evolution and changes occurring in the marketplace, which will then increase the probability of succeeding in uncertain, hypercompetitive environments.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  Overall, does it take more effort to make small, periodic changes or to wait and make more dramatic changes when these become necessary?

 

Who are strategic leaders?

Although dependent upon the size of each organization, most organizations have a Chief Executive Officer (CEO) or top manager and this individual is the primary organizational strategist for that organization. Larger organizations may have a few or several top-level managers, executives, or a top management team. Smaller organizations may have a single individual who fills the role of the chief strategist: the CEO, founder, or owner could all be the same person. What’s important to understand, however, is that no matter what role or position employees hold in an organization, they all can engage in and support the strategic decision making process and need to take responsibility for the strategic actions of their spheres of influence.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  How can you be a strategic leader for your organization?

 

What are the responsibilities of strategic leaders?

Managers at the top of an organization play decisive roles in the entire firms’ efforts to achieve their desired strategic outcomes. As organizational strategists, top managers are primarily responsible for deciding how firm resources will be developed or acquired, at what cost, and how they will be used or allocated throughout the organization. Strategic leaders must also consider the risks of the various actions under consideration and how they align generally with the vision, mission, and objectives of the firm as a whole.

What are the characteristics of strategic leaders?

Although no one “right way” exists for strategic leaders, common characteristics are shared by those who are best suited to succeed in a rapidly changing and evolving environment. Successful strategic leaders rely on thorough analysis before taking action; however, too much time spent assessing all the various elements that could influence a decision could result in “analysis paralysis,” so strategists must balance research with action. As mentioned above, strategic leaders can and should come from various levels throughout the organization, and firms that empower their employees to be involved in strategic decisions that impact their business activities create strategic leaders. Successful leaders should also have goals, objectives, and desires to continually improve the performance of the firm. Leaders who are focused on continual improvement also need to come up with creative and innovative ways to do so, which often involves recognizing or creating opportunities to add value to their customers others don’t or can’t see. Building, cultivating, and leveraging relationships can also be a source of innovative strategic opportunities, so leaders should be willing and able to focus on these relationships both inside and outside the firm. Finally, effective strategic leaders need to have a global mindset to help them understand and anticipate how developments around the globe can be sources of either opportunities for or threats to their strategic activities.

 

 

Reflection Questions: Which of the characteristics of strategic leaders do you possess? Which areas might you need to improve?

 

IMPORTANT ELEMENTS OF THE STRATEGIC MANAGEMENT PROCESS

Typically, strategic management can be divided into two classes: business-level strategy and corporate-level strategy. The latter focuses on what business(es) in which the firm will operate, with the former concerned about how to compete in that selected business. The focus of this course will be on business-level strategy, highlighted by the following topics:

Module 2: Internal organization – vision, mission, resources, capabilities, and core competencies that highlight the firm’s strengths and weaknesses.

Module 3: External environment – trends, forces, and the overall competitive landscape that provide opportunities and threats for the firm’s intended strategy.

Module 4: Business-level Strategy – a chosen set of actions designed to accentuate a firm’s strengths and opportunities while overcoming a firm’s weaknesses and minimizing threats.

Module 5: Competitive Dynamics – a key factor in guiding a firm’s strategic decisions and in determining the effectiveness of a firm’s implemented strategy.

Firms that align their internal environment (Module 2) appropriately with the external environment (Module 3) to formulate and execute a strategy (Module 4) are much more likely to be more successful than their competitors (Module 5).

Mini-case: Blackberry’s Bust

                A pioneer in smartphones, BlackBerry was the undisputed industry leader in the early 2000s, which were preferred by IT managers. Its devices allowed users to receive e-mail and other data in real time globally, with enhanced security features. For executives, a BlackBerry was not just a tool to increase productivity – and to free them from their laptops – but also an important status symbol. As a consequence, by 2008 BlackBerry’s market cap had peaked at $75 billion. Yet by 2015, this valuation had fallen more than 90 percent, to less than $7 billion. What happened?

                The introduction of the iPhone by Apple in 2007 changed the game in the mobile device industry. Equipped with a camera, the iPhone’s slick design offered a user interface with a touchscreen including a virtual keyboard. The iPhone connected seamlessly to other cellular networks and Wi-Fi. Combined with thousands of apps via the Apple iTunes store, the iPhone provided a powerful user experience, or as the late Steve Jobs said, “The Internet in your pocket.”

                However, BlackBerry engineers and executives initially dismissed the iPhone as a mere toy with poor security features. Everyday users thought differently. They has less concern for encrypted software security than they had desire for having fun with a device that allowed them to text, surf the web, take pictures, play games, and do e-mail. Although BlackBerry devices were great in productivity applications, such as receiving and responding to e-mail via typing on its iconic physical keyboard, they provided a poor mobile web browsing experience.

                Initially, mobile devices were issued top-down by corporate IT departments. The only available device for executives was a company-issued BlackBerry. This made life easy for IT departments, ensuring network security. Consumers, however, began to bring their personal iPhones to work and used them for corporate communication and productivity applications. This bottom-up groundswell of the BYOT (“bring your own technology”) movement forced corporate IT departments to open up their services beyond the BlackBerry.

MODULE 2: INTERNAL ORGANIZATION

Strategic leaders need to identify what value can be created by the activities the firm can perform well. Taking stock of characteristics, talents, interests, and abilities – bundled into what is called resources – that can lead to core competencies is an important first step in the strategic decision making process. The Resource-based View (RBV) of the firm can help determine which characteristics can create value and lead to a sustainable competitive advantage.

  2. Describe the differences between tangible and intangible resources.
  3. Define capabilities and discuss their continual development.
  4. Describe four criteria used to determine whether resources and capabilities are core competencies.
  5. Define outsourcing and discuss reasons for its use.

Big Question Question: How does the internal organization of a business influence the strategic management process?

Resource-based View

The Resource-based View (RBV) of the firm was popularized by Jay Barney, an economist who wrote an article in 1991 explaining why firms have different competitive outcomes, which is at the heart of the field of strategic management. His insight was focused inside the firm, namely on the resources each firm possesses, and had two main arguments. The first was the concept of resource heterogeneity, which means that every firm (or person) possesses a unique bundle of resources and capabilities that can be used to form the basis of a sustainable competitive advantage. The second was resource immobility, meaning some resources can’t be easily acquired or transported from one firm to another. These two conditions help explain why firms are so different, particularly in the performance outcomes. How firms can use this uniqueness to form a sustainable competitive advantage is the goal of internal analysis and will be explored in this module.

 

Resources

Resources are defined as inputs into the firm’s production processes, which help the firm create value for its customers. A firm’s competitive advantage is often strongly related to the resources it holds and how they are managed. Resources are the foundation for the strategic decision making process and can generate competitive advantages leading to superior performance when they are bundled together uniquely. As such, strategic decision makers should analyze the internal environment with a focus on understanding how their resources and capabilities might be leveraged and bundled together in a unique, value-adding manner.

Resources are typically classified as either tangible or intangible. Tangible resources are assets that are usually easily observable and quantifiable, and can be classified as follows:

  • Physical – property, equipment, inventory, land
  • Financial – the ability to generate funds through internal business activities or the ability to borrow money
  • Organizational – formal reporting structures and processes

Intangible resources are often more difficult to see and can be deeply rooted in the business’ or individual’s history. Categories of intangible resources include:

  • Human – knowledge, experience, expertise, skills, trust, network connections and relationships, company culture
  • Innovation – capacity to innovate, ideas, creativity
  • Reputational – brand name, brand equity, perceptions of quality, durability, and reliability
  • Intellectual property – protection of intellectual property such as copyrights, patents, trademarks, and trade secrets

 

Of note, the value of tangible resources can often be constrained because they are usually difficult to leverage and to derive additional value. For example, a delivery truck is a tangible resource, but it can only be used on one route in one physical location at a time, and the driver can only be deployed in that one truck at one point in time. Similarly, the financial investment in the delivery truck can only be used for that one truck. Intangible resources, on the other hand, like the ability to innovate a new manufacturing process, can be used on several product lines in multiple locations simultaneously.

  

Reflection question: Do you think tangible or intangible resources are more likely to lead to a sustainable competitive advantage?

 

Sources of Competitive Advantage

Intangible resources are often more difficult to see or understand, which can make them difficult to imitate. In addition, intangible resources, can be the result of unique historical conditions, such as an invention, strategically important location, or other “right place at the right time” situations that gave the firm a head start over other firms. Other intangible resources, such as company culture or network connections, can be socially complex and reliant upon numerous interactions among people inside and outside an organization. The old saying of, “Who you know is more important than what you know” is based on this idea, where often problems are solved because people know who to ask to help solve them rather than simply knowing the answers themselves. Intangible resources can also be causally ambiguous, where the way firms combine and use the various resources to create and deliver value to their customers is not easily identified or understood. Because of the complex and ambiguous nature of intangible resources, they are more difficult to imitate and often form the basis of a sustainable competitive advantage. The importance of imitability will be discussed further below.

 

Capabilities and Core Competencies             

Resources are the building blocks for a firm’s activities, but they alone will generally not be sufficient to provide a firm with a sustainable competitive advantage. The key lies in how those resources are bundled, leveraged, or transformed into capabilities. Capabilities are a firm’s capacity to use individual firm resources to accomplish desired outcomes. These capabilities develop over time as a result of complex interactions between a firm’s tangible and intangible resources, driven by the human capital (people) within the firm. A firm’s ability to achieve a sustainable competitive advantage therefore lies in its knowledge base, ingenuity, and ability of its employees to successfully leverage firm capabilities. The knowledge and ability of the firm’s employees may be one of its most significant sources of competitive advantage because it is a manifestation of everything the firm has learned, and therefore everything it knows about how to successfully combine, link, or bundle sets of individual resources in order to develop and maintain critical capabilities that form the foundation for creating core competencies and, ultimately, a sustainable competitive advantage. As a result, firms need to recognize and support the value employees hold for the organization and ensure they are able to not only establish, but nurture, acquire, and develop new skills to enhance existing capabilities and core competencies. By doing so, firms can continue to evolve and will be more likely to stay ahead of competitors who are also developing their own capabilities.

After a firm understands its resources and capabilities, the next step is to identify its core competencies, which are a subset of capabilities the firm does especially well relative to competitors in the marketplace. These core competencies develop from the resources it acquires and builds and the capabilities it develops and – similar to all of its capabilities – also emerge over time through the process of learning how to combine, develop, and leverage its various resources and capabilities. Through these core competencies, a firm will be able to produce goods or services with features and characteristics that are better, faster, and/or cheaper than rivals and can add unique value to its customers, which will improve its likelihood of success.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What are the core competencies of firms from whom you purchase products and services?

 

 

 

Evaluating Core Competencies for Sustainable Competitive Advantage

Not all resources and capabilities are strategic assets, meaning they have competitive value and the potential to be a basis of a firm’s sustainable competitive advantage. According to the Resource-based View of the firm, four criteria must be satisfied in order to do so, encapsulated by the acronym VRIO. The first is value, where a core competence can either exploit opportunities or minimize threats in the market and must be appreciated by customers such that they are willing to pay for them. The second is rarity, where the core competence is currently possessed by few, if any, competitors in the market. If numerous firms possess the core competence, the likelihood of many firms using them to make the same strategic decisions is high, thus eroding any advantage one firm might have over another. Third, the core competence needs to be costly or difficult to imitate or develop, which helps to protect both its value and rarity. In addition to being directly imitated or copied, the result of the core competence must also be difficult to substitute by other means. Finally, the firm must be organized well to effectively and efficiently utilize the core competence in its business processes.

The internal analysis process, then, takes the core competence through the VRIO sequence to help the firm determine what the strategic and competitive outcome of exploiting a core competence might be. A core competence that is not valuable, if presented to the marketplace, could lead to a competitive disadvantage since no customers would appreciate it or be willing to pay for it. A core competence that is valuable but not rare will not allow the firm to differentiate itself in any meaningful way, which would, at best, lead to competitive parity. A core competence that is valuable and rare but that can be quickly or easily imitated or developed by rival firms will only give the focal firm a temporary competitive advantage, which will only last until customers see the same or similar competence offered by other firms. However, if a core competence is valuable, rare, costly to imitate, and properly used by the firm, it can form a basis of a sustainable competitive advantage.

 

A word of caution: a diligent and successful firm that possesses capabilities and core competencies that once formed the basis of a competitive advantage cannot assume that advantage will last forever. Firms must continually reinvest, hone, and/or upgrade those competencies to help ensure they enjoy a lasting competitive advantage over rivals. Complacency and a set of outdated competencies can become core rigidities, which impede the likelihood of continued success.

Outsourcing

Most firms do not possess all the resources, capabilities, and core competencies needed to be successful in their business endeavors. For those firms that are not able to perform all necessary activities themselves, they can look outside the firm for assistance. Outsourcing is the process of purchasing value-creating activities from an external party and has become an increasingly important activity for three main reasons:

  • The cost and time to develop all of the bundles of resources and capabilities necessary to achieve a sustainable competitive advantage is too high for a firm to do on its own.
  • By focusing on only those few core competencies that can truly lead to a sustainable competitive advantage, firms will be freeing up value time and effort to continue to develop those core competencies rather than be distracted by other, less strategically critical activities.
  • Leveraging other firms’ core competencies can allow the firm access to “world class” activities without having to invest in the cost of developing and maintaining those internally.

 

By concentrating on those areas in which a firm competes best – its core competencies – and outsourcing the rest, firms can increase their flexibility and global competitiveness, and reduce risks by not performing all business activities itself.

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What risks do you see to a firm outsourcing? How would you mitigate those risks?

 

However, outsourcing can have negative consequences as well, so several key issues must be considered to reduce the risks of outsourcing itself. First, firms should not outsource those business activities where the firm itself can create and capture value. Doing so can decrease the ability for the firm to monitor or to learn from that activity, and by providing another firm with the keys to its competitive advantage, the firm could make itself obsolete. Second, firms should not outsource activities that it uses to neutralize environmental threats or to complete necessary ongoing organizational tasks. Not everything should be outsourced, and if the firm is critically dependent on an input to complete its necessary tasks, outsourcing may leave itself vulnerable to the system shutting down. Next, firms should also not outsource capabilities critical to its success, even though the capabilities themselves are not actual sources of competitive advantage. Finally, firms should not outsource activities that stimulate the development of new capabilities and competencies. In a globally competitive and changing environment, learning is critical for ongoing firm success, and removing activities that help it learn could put a firm at a competitive disadvantage in the future.

In order to successfully manage the outsourcing process, skills like deal making/negotiations, relationship development, and monitoring are critical. Deals should be made that benefit both parties, and ensuring trust and dependability in the partnership can help overcome problems that may come up, such as quality control, intellectual property protection, and disruptions that occur outside any one party’s control. Effective monitoring can also help ensure the necessary activities are conducted properly.

Summary

By analyzing the internal capabilities of the firm, the strengths and weaknesses as reflected by their resources, capabilities, and core competencies can be identified. If a firm has weak capabilities or does not possess core competencies in areas deemed necessary to create a competitive advantage, these necessary resources should either be developed or acquired in order to build the needed capabilities and competencies. As areas of strength that could lead to a competitive advantage are identified, they must then pair those with opportunities that exist in the marketplace, which is the topic of the next module.

Mini-case: Internal Organization

Groupon

                After graduating with a degree in music from Northwestern University, Andrew Mason spent a couple of years as a web designer. In 2008, the then 27-year-old founded Groupon, a daily-deal website that connects local retailers and other merchants to consumers by offering goods and services at a discount. Groupon creates marketplaces by bringing the brick-and-mortar world of local commerce onto the Internet. The company basically offers a “group-coupon.” If more than a predetermined number of Groupon users sign up for the offer, the deal is extended to all Groupon users. For example, a local spa may offer a massage for $40 instead of the regular $80. If more than say 10 people sign up, the deal becomes reality. The users prepay $40 for the coupon, which Groupon splits 50-50 with the local merchant. Inspired by how Amazon.com has become the global leader in ecommerce, Mason’s strategic vision for Groupon was to be the global leader in local commerce. To target and fine-tune its local deals, Groupon relies heavily on human labor to do the selling.

                Measured by its explosive growth, Groupon became one of the most successful recent Internet startups, with over 260 million subscribers and serving over 500,000 merchants in the United States and some 50 countries. Indeed, Groupon’s success attracted a $6 billion buyout offer by Google in early 2011, which Mason declined. The spurned Google later created its own daily-deal version with Google offers, and later Amazon (via LivingSocial), and Facebook also moved in. In November 2011, Groupon held a successful initial public offering (IPO), valued at more than $16 billion with a share price of over $26. But a year later, Groupon’s share price had fallen 90 percent to just $2.63, resulting in a market cap of less than $1.8 billion. In early 2013, Mason posted a letter for Groupon employees on the web, arguing that it would leak anyway, stating, “After four and a half intense and wonderful years as CEO of Groupon, I’ve decided that I’d like to spend more time with my family. Just kidding – I was fired today.”

MODULE 3: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT

One of the primary purposes of conducting an external analysis is to identify opportunities and threats. From a strategic standpoint, an opportunity is a condition or problem in the external environment that, if identified and capitalized on effectively, can help a firm provide value to its customers. Threats are conditions that could potentially impair the strategic initiatives and harm the long-term profitability of the firm.

Learning Objectives. By the end of this module, students should be able to:

  1. Explain the importance of analyzing and understanding the firm’s external environment.
  2. Define and describe the general environment and the industry environment.
  3. Name and describe the general environment’s six (plus two) segments.
  4. Identify the five competitive forces and explain how they determine an industry’s profit potential.
  5. Define strategic groups and describe their influence on firms.

Big Question Question: How does the external environment influence the strategic decision making process?

Conditions in the external environment will continue to change, and that change is difficult, if not impossible, to predict or control. As such, firms’ strategic leaders need to be aware of, understand the implications of, and identify patterns represented in these changes by taking actions to improve the firm’s competitive position, to improve operational efficiency, and to be effective global competitors. Understanding the external environment helps build the firm’s base of knowledge and information that can be used to: (1) help build new capabilities and core competencies, (2) buffer the firm from negative environmental impacts, and (3) pursue opportunities to better serve stakeholders’ needs. The strategic challenge is to develop an understanding of the implications of these elements and factors for a firm’s competitive position. Processes and frameworks for the analysis of the external environment are provided in this module.

External analysis efforts should focus on segments most important to the firm’s strategic competitiveness to identify environmental changes, trends, opportunities, and threats that can be matched with the firm’s core competencies so that it can create a sustainable competitive advantage and increase its profitability.

PESTEL Model of the General Environment

The general environment is composed of elements in the broader society that can indirectly influence an industry and the firms within an industry. These elements can be classified using the PESTEL model as follows:

Political relate to policies instituted by government agencies, such as increased regulations on imports and exports, natural resource prices, immigration policies, free trade vs. protectionism, and subsidies.

 

Economic refer to the nature and direction of the economy in which a firm competes or may compete from a macro level. This includes issues such as national income levels/disparity, personal savings rates, inflation rates and interest rates, trade deficits and surpluses, budget deficits and surpluses, gross domestic product, currency valuation and fluctuation, unemployment rates, higher disposable incomes, and population of dual wage-earner families)

Sociocultural involves different societies’ social attitudes and cultural values preferences, to include demographics such as age, ethnicity, and population size, attitudes towards health and fitness, and increasing interactions via social media.

Technological are the institutions and activities involved with creating new knowledge and translating that knowledge into new outputs, products, processes, and materials. These can include the use of mobile technology, e-commerce and other Internet advances, and increasing plant automation.

Environmental refers to potential and actual changes in the physical environment such as climate change, seasonality of demand and production, energy consumption, and sustainability.

Legal involve judiciary processes prevalent in each geographic location, which include the various laws and regulations governing business activities such as anti-trust provisions, protection of property rights (including intellectual property), extradition, and precedent and history of enforcement.

 

 

Two potentially important missing elements from the popular PESTEL framework are:

 

International are activities from all over the world, including cultural differences, innovations, and practices in other countries.

Competitive are how rivals act and respond to include innovations from rivals in the same industry, innovations in other industries, the speed of market adoption and imitation, among others. These competitive forces are discussed further in the next section and in Module 5.

 

Often, firms cannot directly control the general environment’s elements but must instead understand these elements in order to make better strategic decisions.

Industry Analysis – Porter’s Five Forces

An industry is a group of firms that provide goods and services that are close substitutes for each other to similar customers. As firms in the same industry compete for market share and overall profitability, the strategies implemented by these companies influence each other and include a broad mix of competitive strategies. Whereas factors in the general environment discussed above tend to have an indirect effect on strategic formulation and implementation, the effects of the industry environment tend to be much more direct. As a result, industry and individual firm profitability tend to be influenced by five competitive forces, called Porter’s Five Forces.

Named after Michael Porter, Harvard professor and the “Godfather” of Strategic Management, Five Forces analysis usually has a more direct impact on the types of competitive actions and reactions a firm will take in a given industry. Essentially, Five Forces analysis examines competition from a more holistic view, where different actors in a given industry – or a specific part of an industry – are competing for profitability. Five Forces analysis is also widely used to determine the overall attractiveness of one industry versus another, where an industry in which the Forces are strong is usually less profitable. It is also used to identify the threats and opportunities within various parts of the industry value chain.

The Five Forces of interest are: bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, intensity of rivalry, threat of substitutes, and threat of new entrants. In essence, numerous actors in the industry environment can be considered “competitors” for profitability as firms interact to produce goods and services. So even though suppliers, for example, are partners and integral in providing necessary raw materials for the firm to use in production, in a zero sum environment, the higher prices a supplier charges, the more expensive the focal firm’s production process will be, which will ultimately decrease overall firm profitability. Similarly, the lower the price customers pay for the firm’s goods and services, the lower the profitability for the firm. In this regard, the various players in an industry are competing for circumstances that benefit their individual firm, which result in higher performance and profitability.

Another thing to keep in mind is the dynamic nature of industry players, where current suppliers could become direct competitors by integrating forward, current buyers (customers) could become competitors by integrating backwards, and firms that are not competitors today could produce products that serve as substitutes for existing products offered by firms in an industry, transforming themselves into competitors. In addition, new competitors can come from international sources, so maintaining a global mindset (and mindfulness) is important when forecasting the evolution of an industry.

 

Further discussion of each of the Five Forces is as follows:

The bargaining power of suppliers is powerful when firm profitability is reduced by its suppliers’ actions. Suppliers can exert their power by raising prices or by restricting the quantity and/or quality of the goods they provide. Their power is generally stronger when few suppliers exist or the product is unique or highly specialized, since their product is more rare and valuable. The more important the suppliers’ good or services are to the focal firm and the higher its switching costs (meaning the financial, cognitive, or psychological/emotional expenditures incurred for a customer to switch to a different supplier), the stronger the power of the suppliers. Generally, the power of suppliers is increased when:

  • The supplier segment of the industry is dominated by a few large companies and is more concentrated than the industry to which it sells
  • Satisfactory substitute products are not available to industry firms
  • Industry firms are not a significant customer group for the supplier group
  • Suppliers’ goods are critical to buyers’ marketplace success
  • Effectiveness or uniqueness of suppliers’ products has created high switching costs for buyers
  • Suppliers represent a credible threat to integrate forward into the buyers’ industry, especially when suppliers have substantial resources and provide highly differentiated products

For the bargaining power of buyers, customers are usually interested in purchasing products at the lowest possible price, which in turn provides a lower level of profitability for the selling firm (or supplier). To reduce cost or to maximize value, customers will typically bargain for higher quality or greater levels of service, which is usually more expensive for selling firms, and increases competition among firms in the industry. The exact opposite of the power of suppliers, if few buyers exist, the focal firm’s products or services are not specialized or unique and not important to the buyer, and the lower their switching costs, the stronger the power of the buyers. For both of these, the higher the likelihood of vertical integration – either forward for the suppliers or backwards for the buyers – the more power each group has. Generally, the power of buyers is increased when:

  • Buyers are important to sellers because they purchase a large portion of the supply industry’s total sales
  • Products purchased represent a significant portion of the seller’s annual revenues
  • Buyers are able to switch to another supplier’s product at little, if any, cost
  • Suppliers’ products are undifferentiated and standardized, and
  • Buyers represent a real threat to integrate backward into the suppliers’ industry using resources or expertise

Leveraging the vast amount of information about manufacturer’s costs and numerous alternatives available on the internet, consumers are increasing their bargaining power in many industries. Switching costs are also dropping when customers decide to purchase from one manufacturer rather than another or from one seller as opposed to a second or third one.

The intensity of rivalry in an industry is often higher when the concentration of the industry is high, meaning one firm or a small group of firms can yield high market power. This also occurs when the competitors are relatively equally balanced. When an industry is highly fragmented with numerous smaller firms, certain pockets of the industry might see strong rivalry, but the industry as a whole is not dominated by any one firm or small groups of firms. When industry growth is slow and/or has high fixed costs, firms try to maximize the use of their productive capacity and will fight over a “fixed pie” of existing customers, which will also tend to increase rivalry. Similarly, when industry players have a lack of differentiation in their products or services and buyers have low switching costs, intensity of competition will increase for the existing customer base and rivalry will tend to focus on price rather than customer service or the underlying characteristics of the product or service. A further exploration into competitive dynamics between firms is in Module 5.

The threat of substitutes is generally high when customers’ needs can be satisfied with other products or services that come from outside the industry. This threat is increased when switching costs for customers are low and when the substitute product’s price is lower or its quality and characteristics are greater than or equal to those of the focal firm’s products. In effect, the prices charged for substitutes represent the maximum firms in the focal industry can charge for their products and services, and the substitutes’ characteristics or quality set the floor for the firms’ products and services. Firms can offset the relative attractiveness of substitutes by differentiating their products and services in ways that are of value to customers, which can include price, quality, features, location, and/or level of service.

Finally, the threat of new entrants can harm incumbent firms by either introducing innovative new products or production processes or by increasing supply without increasing the level of demand, which will tend to lower industry prices and thus profitability. The threat of new entrants is usually a function of barriers to entry, which include:

  • Economies of scale, where the price per unit decreases as more units are produced, which provides a cost advantage to the incumbent firm. Potential new entrants must therefore produce at a high level to achieve these cost advantages or by producing multiple products at once.
  • Product differentiation makes it hard for new firms to compete without comparable product characteristics. In addition, the brand loyalty and reputation incumbent firms have achieved take a large investment and a lot of time, which put new entrants at a further disadvantage.
  • Capital requirements that require high startup or maintenance costs for property, plant, equipment, and other resources for new entrants also increase risk and commitment levels.
  • Switching costs make it expensive or difficult for customers to adopt new products and services.
  • Access to distribution channels should not be assumed to exist equally for all firms in an industry, and new firms may incur high costs finding effective means to sell and distribute their products and services.
  • Cost advantages independent of scale, which are similar to “unique historical conditions” where incumbent firms may have proprietary process (or product) technology, more favorable access to or control of raw materials, the best locations, brand recognition, favorable government subsidies, and so forth.
  • Government policy, where the number of competitors is actually restricted through licensing and permit requirements at various levels of government (federal, state, and local).

When barriers to entry are high, the threat of new entrants tends to be lower. Firms trying to enter an industry should make strategic decisions that lower the barriers to entry, while those firms already in a given industry should make strategic decisions that increase barriers to entry.

An effective Five Forces analysis helps entrepreneurs identify both the attractiveness of an industry as a whole as well as potentially profitable opportunities that might exist within an industry. By recognizing these opportunities, alert firms can combine existing industry-relevant trends with creative problem solving to add value in a unique way as a means to enter and to successfully compete in a given industry. In general, Porter’s Five Forces have an inverse relationship with profitability in an industry, where the stronger the competitive forces, the lower the profit potential for an industry’s firms. An unattractive industry has low entry barriers, suppliers and buyers with strong bargaining positions, strong competitive threats from product substitutes, and intense rivalry among competitors, which make it difficult for firms to achieve strategic competitiveness and earn above-average returns. An attractive industry has the mirror image of these features and offers more potential for favorable performance.

Strategic Groups

Within each industry, all firms are not homogeneous. As with individuals, each firm has its own set of unique characteristics, which it uses to attempt to create value better, faster, and/or cheaper than rivals. However, firms can be categorized across certain dimensions that help form clusters or groups of firms. A subset of firms within an industry that emphasize similar strategic dimensions and therefore employ similar strategies are called strategic groups, which can help firms analyze and understand how industry dynamics work. Because members within a strategic group attempt to solve customers’ problems in very similar ways using similar strategies, competition within strategic groups tends to be stronger than between such groups. Further, because the underlying characteristics and strategies of the various groups are different, they are impacted differently by external environmental factors (think PESTEL) as well as by Porter’s Five Forces; as a result, some groups face different opportunities and threats and are thus are more or less profitable than others. Changing underlying characteristics and subsequent strategies is not easy, however, which creates mobility barriers that make it difficult for firms from one strategic group to move to another group. The better a firm understands how the dynamics of an industry work, the better able it is to identify opportunities to compete in a given industry.

 

SWOT Analysis

A framework that ties internal analysis with external analysis is called SWOT, which stands for Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. Strengths and weaknesses are usually identified during internal analysis, while opportunities and threats are a result of external analysis. Firms operating in or trying to enter an industry should combine its strengths – manifested by core competencies able to form the foundation of a sustainable competitive advantage – with opportunities that exist in the marketplace. The better the alignment, the better the likelihood of success. Being aware of threats to incumbent firms can help the firm find weaknesses in the market to exploit, while also being aware of its own weaknesses that leave it vulnerable to competitive activity by rivals and changing customer preferences.

MODULE 4: BUSINESS-LEVEL STRATEGIES

Due to the intense nature of competition and the changing external forces in the business environment, firms need to make sure their strategies leverage their strengths with existing opportunities. Similarly, a firm’s strategy needs to help overcome weaknesses and minimize threats in order to be successful. The building of a firm’s business-level strategy must align internal and external characteristics to help maximize its likelihood of achieving a sustainable competitive advantage.

Learning Objectives. By the end of this module, students should be able to:

  1. Define business-level strategy and explain the differences between them
  2. Apply the Five Forces model to each business-level strategy
  3. Describe the advantages and disadvantages of each type of business-level strategy

Big Question Question: How does a firm leverage its internal and external analysis to choose an appropriate business-level strategy? What are the different types of strategies available to firms to help them achieve a sustainable competitive advantage?

This module focuses on the “how” of satisfying customer needs, which translates into business-level strategies. The purpose of a business-level strategy is to leverage the strengths of a firm matched with a promising opportunity in the external environment in order to provide unique value above and beyond what is offered by other firms. In essence, business-level strategies help the firm to either do things other firms are doing differently (such as at a lower cost with a lower price point) or to do completely different things (such as differentiating quality, features, or characteristics). Relatedly, two “generic” strategies identified in strategic management are cost leadership and differentiation. The better the firm is able to align the firm’s core competencies and customer needs with an appropriate strategy, the better the likelihood of creating a sustainable competitive advantage.

Customer Focus

When deciding what strategy to follow, firms must keep the needs and wants of the customer as the central point of its strategic decision making process. A firm’s relationships with its customers will be strengthened when it provides superior value to them. This customer focus highlights the following questions firms must simultaneously consider throughout this process:

 

  • Whom to serve,
  • What customer needs or preferences will be satisfied,
  • Which core competencies are necessary to satisfy customer needs, and
  • How those needs will be satisfied through the strategy selected.

 

When identifying the customers to serve, the first step is to classify characters based on differences in needs or preferences, which is called market segmentation. This allows firms to come up with strategies that will address each segment and to also identify qualities that span multiple segments. By doing so, firms can allocate resources more efficiently and effectively to meet the needs of its various customers. Dimensions that can be used to identify customer segments include demographic, socioeconomic, geographic, psychological, and perceptual factors, as well as consumption patterns.

Determining which customer needs to satisfy is important because successful firms learn how to deliver to customers what, when, and where they want it. Customers’ needs or wants are generally satisfied by the product’s characteristics and features, so having frequent and close interactions with both current and potential customers helps the firm better understand what needs the customers want satisfied and can integrate them into their research and development and marketing activities. In addition, successful firms are also able to anticipate changes in customers’ needs and preferences, and by the process of continuous innovation and improvement, can repeatedly provide exceptional value for which customers are willing to pay.

Core competencies are resources and capabilities that serve as a basis for a firm’s sustainable competitive advantage relative to its rivals. Successful firms leverage those core competencies to create and implement value-adding strategies that satisfy customer needs and preferences. Firms that align their core competencies with customer needs, and then continuously improve, innovate, and upgrade their competencies accordingly, have the potential to meet and exceed customers’ expectations over time. In that way, the sustainability of a firm’s competitive advantage is a function of the alignment of core competencies and customer needs combined with continual improvement.

Business-level Strategies

Business-level strategies represent coordinated sets of actions that leverage core competencies to create a competitive advantage. An effectively formulated strategy integrates and allocates a firm’s resources, capabilities, and competencies to be properly aligned with its external environment. As such, business-level strategies indicate how a firm chooses to compete in a particular market to gain a competitive advantage over rivals in the marketplace. Business-level strategies create differences between the firm’s competitive position and those of its rivals as a firm must decide whether it intends to perform the same activities differently or to perform different activities altogether.

Generic Strategies

Classified as “generic” because they can be applied across most industries, products, and public or private sectors, these strategies are situated on a graph based on source of competitive advantage – either uniqueness or cost – and the scope of operations – either broad or narrow. Obviously, the effectiveness of each strategy depends on the alignment between internal and external conditions and how the firm executes that strategy. No one strategy is superior to another in all circumstances, and within each industry (similar to strategic groups) firms are able to be successful by following vastly different strategies. The two most prominent business-level strategies, cost leadership and differentiation, will be discussed in detail next.

 

 

 

 

Cost Leadership

As the name suggests, a cost leadership strategy emphasizes actions to produce goods or services with features valued by customers at the lowest cost relative to competitors. The cost leadership strategy usually requires standardized goods or services and process innovations designed to produce and distribute goods or services efficiently. This includes supply chain management that emphasizes partnerships with producers and streamlined distribution channels that help keeps costs as low as possible. Cost leaders also need to routinely examine all business activities to uncover additional areas for improvement and cost reductions, which could include heavy investment in state-of-the-art manufacturing facilities and/or logistical processes. Customers targeted by a cost leadership strategy tend to be very price conscious and may value a low price above most other concerns.

Firms following a cost leadership strategy successfully typically focus on the following areas:

 

  • Simplification of processes and procedures
  • Achieving efficiency and effectiveness
  • Reducing costs throughout all levels of the organization
  • Monitoring costs of activities provided by external parties that interface with the firm’s inbound or outbound logistics

Cost Leadership and Five Forces

As an example of the impact strategic decision makers can have on the processes and outcomes of the firm, the following section highlights how a cost leadership strategy can minimize or reduce the influence of Porter’s Five Forces.

Power of Suppliers: Low-cost leaders can absorb a greater amount of cost increases from powerful suppliers before it must raise prices charged to customers. In addition, combined with dominant market share, a low-cost leader may be in a position to force suppliers to reduce prices or to hold down the level of price increases, and thus reduce the power of suppliers.

Power of Buyers: the lowest cost producer has a larger cushion between the price it charges for its goods or services and the cost to produce those goods and services, so if buyers put pressure on the price it charges, it can still remain profitable. In addition, if buyers attempt to drive prices below the cost of the next most efficient firm, that firm might choose to exit the market (rather than remain and earn below average profits), leaving the low cost firm with a monopoly position. If that happens, customers would lose any bargaining power as the monopoly firm would be in a position to raise prices.

Intensity of Rivalry: a firm’s rivals will hesitate to compete based on price because, in a price war, the low cost firm has a bigger cushion between its price and costs, which will allow it to still earn a profit longer that rivals.

Threat of Substitutes: Low-cost leaders can more easily lower prices to maintain the price-value relationship with current customers and reduce the attractiveness of substitute goods and services.

Threat of New Entrants: Low-cost leaders typically must produce and sell in large volumes and focus on efficiency and cost reduction, so new must either enter the industry at a large scale (large enough to achieve the same economies of scale as the next lowest cost firm) or be satisfied with average profits until they move sufficiently far down the experience curve to match the efficiencies of the low-cost leader.

Several competitive risks of a cost leadership strategy also exist as firms are always susceptible to the strategic actions of rivals. First, the processes used by the cost leader may become obsolete such that the cost advantage of the firm would erode based on newer technologies. Rivals may also catch up to the cost leader and leverage their own scale advantages to reduce the power of the former cost leader. Rivals might also find a way to imitate or substitute for the cost leader’s strategic advantage by using its own core competencies. Further, the pursuit of a cost leadership strategy may occur at the expense of customers’ perceptions of value, where the goods or services provided are seen as “cheap” rather than “inexpensive.” Lastly, the cost leader might suffer from a lack of identification of changes in customer needs and preferences and get left behind by those changing trends.

 

 

Differentiation

A differentiation strategy emphasizes actions to produce goods or services (at an acceptable cost) with characteristics customers perceive as offering greater quality, features, and/or value relative to competitors. Although with any business endeavor, cost is certainly a matter of concern, but the focus of the differentiation strategy is to make something unique and special such that customers would be willing to pay a premium for it. Competition, then, is not focused on the price of the product but rather what it does that is superior to other products or services. Differentiation can come from various sources, including but not limited to, high quality, unusual, unique, or additional features, responsive customer service, rapid product innovation, advanced technological features, high performance, and an image of prestige or status.

In order to successfully implement a differentiation strategy, high investment in activities such as research and development, innovation, and marketing is usually required. The creativity and ingenuity of the firm is also important to produce a solution to customers’ problems in new and innovative ways. Customers targeted by a differentiation strategy usually do not place price as the highest priority but rather are more concerned about the functionality and quality of the product or service and how it solves their particular problem better than other alternatives. As such, they are generally willing to pay a higher price, thus allowing the firm to earn a profit with higher margins.

Differentiation and Five Forces

As an example of the impact strategic decision makers can have on the processes and outcomes of the firm, the following section highlights how a differentiation strategy can minimize or reduce the influence of Porter’s Five Forces.

 

Power of Suppliers: Differentiation often allows firms to charge higher prices, thus making large margins, which puts them in a better position to absorb any price increases from suppliers. In addition, because of the lower price sensitivity from customers due to high brand loyalty, firms can pass along any price increases caused by higher supplier prices.

Power of Buyers: Differentiated products are often unique, which helps reduce customer sensitivity to instances of price increases, shipping delays, or other such inconveniences.

Intensity of Rivalry: A differentiation strategy often results in high brand loyalty, which helps the firm maintain an advantage over rivals’ products or services.

Threat of Substitutes: A differentiation strategy allows firms to more easily lower prices to maintain the price-value relationship with current customers and reduce the attractiveness of substitute goods and services. In addition, the characteristics of the differentiated product increase the switching costs between other products or services.

Threat of New Entrants: The high customer loyalty resulting from a uniquely differentiated product or service would take a lot of time and investment for a new entrant to achieve, who must either overcome or surpass the uniqueness of existing products or provide similarly differentiated products at a lower price to increase the value provided to the customer.

Several competitive risks also exist for a differentiation strategy. First, although the brand loyalty that often comes with a differentiation strategy reduces customers’ sensitivity to price, it is still a contributing factor to a perception of value, and when the price differential between the differentiator’s product or service and the cost leader’s becomes too large, the value of the former is diminished. As a result, the differentiation ceases to provide value for which the customers are willing to pay. Further, as customers gain experience with the product and perhaps other rivals’ goods or substitute products, the value of the differentiated features is narrowed. Finally, the price premium of differentiated products is eroded when counterfeit goods replicate the differentiated features of the firm’s products.

Blue Ocean Strategy

A relatively recent strategic theory builds upon the “integrated” strategy of cost leadership and differentiation, but instead focuses on how the strategic actions by firms can completely change the nature of competition – and profitability – in entire industries. The Blue Ocean Strategy, introduced by Kim & Mauborgne in 2005, uses an analogy of an ocean to describe competitive behavior between firms. When these firms (sharks) compete directly, blood is in the water and no firm really has a sustainable advantage over the others. Instead, they suggest implementing “value innovation” and swimming to blue oceans where no other sharks (firms) are competing, which changes market boundaries and industry structures. Value innovation means increasing value for customers by creating new benefits and services, while simultaneously reducing costs by eliminating less valuable features or services, which rejects the traditionally accepted trade-off between differentiation and lower cost. The “Four Actions Framework” details how this can be accomplished.

Four actions framework:

  1. Eliminate: Which of the factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
  2. Reduce: Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
  3. Raise: Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
  4. Create: Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

 

RESPONSE_QUESTION:  What firms can you think of that have successfully implemented a Blue Ocean Strategy?

 

 

Successfully implementing the Blue Ocean Strategy takes a special ability to see opportunities where others do not. By building capabilities in these new markets, firms are able to innovate in a way that adds value by combining both differentiation and low cost strategies. One key to success with this strategy is recognizing the current benefits and pain points of the customer experience and creating a product or service that maximizes the benefits and reduces the pain points to create a sustainable competitive advantage. The results of Blue Ocean strategies tend to be more radical and disruptive in nature and, if done properly, can make it difficult for competitors to imitate, protecting the firm’s competitive advantage. Another positive outcome of a successful Blue Ocean strategy is attracting an entirely new set of customers that weren’t interested in previous offerings; by doing so, firms can expand the potential market and help reduce head-to-head competition for a previously limited market boundary.

JetBlue

                Entrepreneur David Neeleman, at the age of 25, co-founded Morris Air, a charter air service that in 1993 was purchased by Southwest Airlines (SWA). Morris Air was a low-fare airline that pioneered many cost-saving practices that later became standard in the industry, such as e-ticketing. After working as an airline executive for SWA, Neeleman founded another low-cost airline, JetBlue Airways, in 1998. At the same time, he also wanted to offer better service and more amenities.

                JetBlue started by using just one type of airplane (the Airbus A320) and chose to fly point to point, directly connecting highly trafficked city pairs. In contrast, legacy airlines such as Delta, United, and American use a hub-and-spoke system; such systems connect many different locations via layovers at airport hubs. The point-to-point business model focuses on directly connecting fewer but more highly trafficked city pairs.

                Some of JetBlue’s other features include high-end 100-seat Embraer regional jets with leather seats, free movie and television programming via DirecTV, XM Satellite Radio, along with friendly and attentive on-board service. Other amenities include its recently added Mint class, which offers personal check-in and early boarding, free bag checking and priority bag retrieval after flight, and complimentary gourmet food and alcoholic beverages in flight. It also features small private suites with a lie-flat bed up to 6 feet 8 inches long, a 15-inch high-resolution personal screen, and free in-flight high-speed Wi-Fi (“Fly-Fi”). JetBlue is also adding the newer Airbus 321 to its fleet, which scores significantly higher in customer satisfaction surveys than the older Airbus 320.

                Also, because roughly one-third of customers prefer speaking to a live reservation agent, despite a highly functional website for reservations and other travel-related services, JetBlue decided to employ stay-at-home parents in the United States instead of following industry best practices by outsourcing its reservation system to India. The company suggests this “home sourcing” is more productive than outsourcing; it also says that customers’ appreciation of the reservation experience more than makes up for the wage differential between the United States and India. To sum it up, JetBlue’s “Customer Bill of Rights” declare its dedication to “bringing humanity back to air travel.”

TOPIC 5: COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS

Competition is an important part of any business activity, as others in the market are motivated to earn a profit, often from selling goods and services to the very same customers as other firms.

Learning Objectives: At the end of this module, students should be able to:

 

  1. Define competitive dynamics
  2. Explain who competitors are and how they can be identified
  3. Describe conditions that influence the level of rivalry among competitors

Big Question Question: How does the nature of competition influence the strategic management process?

 

Defining Competition

The implementation of strategic decisions are not done in a vacuum, and the success of a strategic action is partly the result of how and when rivals respond to that action. Firms that operate in the same market with similar products aimed at similar customer groups are called competitors. Competitive behavior is the repeated actions and reactions firms take to build, defend, or improve its market position in an effort to gain and sustain a competitive advantage. Firms are engaged in multimarket competition when they compete against each other in numerous product or geographic markets, and in an increasingly global marketplace, firms need to consider firms across the world in their competitive analysis. The field of competitive dynamics looks at the series of actions and reactions taken by all firms competing in a market and tries to better understand and predict the dynamic competitive behavior of firms.

 

 

Competition can be good, since it can demonstrate market demand, validate business models, and drive innovation for the firm and value creation for customers. Often a firm must continue to innovate and improve its product/service offerings to keep up and surpass its competitors. From this perspective, competition is a positive thing because it keeps the firm sharp.

Carl Karcher, founder of the Carl’s Jr. hamburger chain, had this to say about competition: “I had always said that competition just makes you stronger. You shouldn’t be afraid of the competition. They make you stay on top of your game. If another chain started near a Carl’s Jr., it showed we had a good location and it brought more people in . . . I may have had a different philosophy than some of the others, but I believe that your competitors are really your friends. They keep you on your toes.” In-N-Out Burger, Chapter 3.        

No competition is not always a good thing. Perhaps a market doesn’t exist, the economics are such that it’s not really a good opportunity, or maybe an effective business model doesn’t exist for the firm. So even though no competition has been described as a potential Blue Ocean opportunity, it could very well be no sharks are swimming there for a reason.

Who Are Your Competitors?

 

Before understanding how to compete with rivals, identifying who your rivals are becomes a critical first step. Competition can be organized into three types. The first is direct competition where rivals are easily defined, offer similar types of products that solve the same problem, and have a comparable resource base. An example of this is Pepsi and Coke, who except for the most die hard of soda aficionados, are more or less indistinguishable. The next is indirect competition where rivals are less easily identifiable. For example, Coke and bottled water could be in this category, since both are beverages but satisfy thirst in different ways. Coke has diversified by launching Dasani, its own bottled water brand, to enter this segment of the market. Finally remote competition is a category where rivals are based on solving a particular problem rather than the specific characteristics of the product. So for Coke, fresh fruit might be a remote competitor for those who want something sweet and will choose between either a soda or fruit. Ideologies might also be at competition here, since customers trying to eat healthily might choose fruit over a carbonated beverage. The key here is to get into the minds of the customers to understand what problem they are trying to solve and to make sure the firm is paying attention to trends and potential threats, both inside and outside the current industry.

The AMC Model of Competitive Rivalry

Competitive rivalry exists when firms interact with one another to pursue an advantageous market position. The factors that influence the timing, intensity, duration, and overall number of competitive actions and reactions have several different dimensions. One popular framework for understanding and predicting competitive dynamics is the awareness, motivation, capabilities (AMC) perspective. The AMC framework argues a firm is more likely to respond to a competitive action by its rival to the extent it is aware of the action, is motivated to respond, and has the capabilities to do so. Awareness is the attention paid to certain rivals, who may either have a reputation as a strategic threat or is a sufficient enough player for a firm to monitor. Sometimes firms suffer from competitive blind spots by not paying close enough attention to actions by lesser-known rivals; when these actions fit the preferences of customers better than current product or service offerings, even well-established firms can be disrupted and go bankrupt. Motivation to respond to a competitor’s attack is increased when market dependence is high, meaning the product or geographic arena is strategically important, either from a profitability perspective, locational advantage, or some other vital operational purpose. Capabilities to respond are built up over time and depend on the investment made into organizational resources.

The AMC perspective is evident when analyzing the market commonality and resource similarity between two firms. Market commonality represents the degree to which firms compete in the same market or markets, including product markets and geographic markets. Using the airline industry as an example, each city to which the airline flies can be considered a market; thus, more market commonality exists as the number of cities two airlines service increases, with an accompanying increase in competitive behavior. Because these firms engage in multimarket competition, they are more aware of each other’s strategic actions and are more likely to respond to them. This increase in the frequency of interaction provides firms with a history of one another, which can help them predict what a firm will do based on that past history. Increased levels of competitive interaction also increase the motivation for firms to respond quickly and intensely to competitive attacks to not only reduce the advantage the initial action produced, but also to deter similar attacks in the other markets the firms share.

One perspective that is an extension of market commonality argues for reduced incentive for firms to initiate competitive rivalry. The mutual forbearance theory suggests firms that are involved in multimarket competition will be less likely to engage in a competitive attack because of the numerous markets in which a retaliation could be expected. However, once attacked, this theory predicts an intense reaction to protect the multiple markets in which the firms compete.

Resource similarity refers to the extent to which both the type and amount a firm’s tangible and intangible resources are comparable to a competitor’s. Firms with similar types and amounts of resources are likely to have similar strengths and weaknesses and to use similar strategies when faced with similar external conditions. As such, the firms are more likely to develop comparable capabilities, which means they are more apt to compete in the marketplace. In contrast to market commonality, assessing resource similarity can be difficult, especially for critical resources that are intangible (e.g., brand name, knowledge, trust, capacity to innovate) rather than tangible (e.g., access to raw materials, ability to borrow capital).

The AMC perspective also helps to explain competitive dynamics with other firm characteristics that manifest themselves from resource similarity, such as firm size. Larger firms may not have the awareness or motivation to compete directly against smaller firms, as smaller firms can often fly below the radar of larger firms. In turn, smaller firms don’t have the necessary resources or capabilities to compete directly with larger firms. Thus the AMC perspective would predict the speed, intensity, and duration of competitive actions and reactions will be higher with firms of similar size.

Strategic and Tactical Actions

 

In the field of competitive dynamics, competitive actions usually take one of two forms. The first is strategic actions, which involve significant commitment or organizational resources and are difficult to implement or reverse. Examples of strategic actions include mergers and acquisitions, significant long-term investments in research and development, and international expansion. Tactical actions, on the other hand, involve less commitment and fewer resources and are relatively easier to implement or reverse. Price changes, marketing campaigns, and the hiring of new employees are examples of tactical actions. Firms use both strategic and tactical actions when forming their competitive actions and competitive responses during their strategic decision making process.

 

 

Effect of Competitive Rivalry

 

Simply choosing an effective strategy based on internal and external analysis is not enough. As this module illustrates, the actions and reactions of rivals in the marketplace have a big impact on the success of the strategy chosen and implemented. So not only is the success of a strategy determined by the firm’s initial competitive actions, but also by how well the firm anticipates competitors’ responses to them and how well those actions respond to previous actions taken by rivals. Competitive dynamics has a dominant influence on the firm’s choice of an appropriate business-level strategy and the effectiveness of its implementation.

Mini-case: Competitive Dynamics

 

Blockbuster

                Netflix provides rental DVDs through the mail rather than via the bricks-and-mortar rental outlets favored by Blockbuster. Blockbuster has built a competitive advantage on the premise that customers can get DVDs they want immediately and often have multiple copies of popular titles to ensure no stock out occurs. Despite this, the founders of Netflix realized that Blockbuster’s customers had points of pain: they had to drive to the store, search through rows of movies, often not find the one they were seeking as it was already rented out, and pay late charges if they forgot to return it on time. Netflix solved these points of pain by mailing the movies directly to the customer and movies can be kept as long as a customer wishes with no late charges.

                When John Antioco, CEO of Blockbuster, first encountered Netflix, he did not see it as a threat, stating, “No one will want to wait three days for a movie.” In fact, having a wish list and allowing a subscriber to hold several DVDs simultaneously avoids this supposed disadvantage. And because inventory is stored centrally, a greater selection is possible at a much lower cost. The Netflix business model innovation is “disruptive” to Blockbuster, which has invested much in stores and local inventories. Blockbuster countered the competitive actions of Netflix by offering its own movie-by-mail system through its existing website called Blockbuster Online that allowed customers to order DVDs to receive through the mail and (for higher-tiered customers) provide coupons for free in-store rentals. Initially, the price for Blockbuster Online and Netflix was the same, but Blockbuster eventually had to raise its prices to cover the additional costs of owning and operating physical stores. This strategy ultimately failed, causing Blockbuster to declare bankruptcy and to close all but one of its stores.

                Eventually, Netflix began seeing an impact of a disruptive technology on its own business through the evolution of broadband networks that can deliver on-demand streamed content. This allowed new competitors to enter the market and challenge Netflix. In addition, competitors in the physical DVD space also began to emerge. Redbox, with its a vending machine style delivery system and flexible pricing system, found a cheaper way to distribute physical DVDs to satisfy customers’ impulse to watch a movie without having to manage a queue or wait for it to be delivered. Although still charging late fees, not as numerous as Blockbuster stores once were, and not able to provide the same breadth of selection, Redbox kiosks are located in heavily trafficked areas to help satisfy customers’ need for convenience, providing an alternative to Netflix’s movie-by-mail system.
